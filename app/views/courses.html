Undergraduate Courses

Computer-Assisted Data Analysis I

This course was offered for undergraduates in the Culture and Communication department at Drexel University. Students learned basic statistics concepts and also learned to design quantitative research studies and use SPSS to manage and analyze their data.

Writing from Sources III: Meeting the Reading and Writing Challenges on Campus

This academic English course is required for international undergraduate students at the University of Washington who do not pass out of it. It is also open to graduate and professional students from a variety of schools.


Graduate Courses

Language for Specific Purposes

Language for Specific Purposes offers international students a hands-on introduction to the practices that constitute academic language use at GSE. Through small projects, readings, and discussions, students will investigate how academic English is actually used in GSE classes and for various assignments in the TESOL and Intercultural Communication programs. In particular, students will focus on developing skills and strategies that will strengthen their existing expertise in the following areas: locating, reading and critiquing academic articles; producing graduate-level written work across a variety of genres; and participating in oral activities.

Understanding Language Learning

This course is part of a four-course sequence for those interested in obtaining the ESL Program Specialist Certificate in Pennsylvania. This course addresses the processes of language learning from a variety of perspectives and engages students in an understanding of the structure and sound systems of English in relation to other languages and in relation to a variety of teaching strategies. By focusing both on a comparison of language structures and sound systems as well as theories of second language acquisition and development, this course allows students to learn about differences between learning a first and a second language and the influences of these processes on instructional principles and strategies. This course includes an intensive language learning component for students in the course that will allow them to reflect on their own learning processes in relation to language learning theories and to compare English to another language.


Teacher Training Courses

ACT 48 Workshops

Weekend workshops for ESL teachers at the Nationalities Service Center in Philadelphia. Students were volunteer teachers at the Center or K-12 teachers who were seeking Act 48 credits. Workshops included Introduction to Linguistics for Teaching ESL; How Languages of the World Work: Implications for Teaching ESL; and Dialects of American English: Teaching Students to Understand and Communicate Effectively with a Variety of English Speakers in the USA.

TESOL Workshop

An intensive three-day, fifteen-hour introduction to Teaching English to Speakers of Other Languages offered throughout the year by the University of Pennsylvania Graduate School of Education. Lectures and practical hands-on sessions covered topics in teaching sounds and structures of English, TESOL methods, developing materials for ESL/EFL, planning lessons, language assessment, teaching English abroad, and employment in the TESOL profession. Workshop was taught in Philadelphia, Taiwan & Korea.


Languages Courses - (ESL/EFL)

Intermediate Level 700 Core Course (All Skills)

This course prepares students to make the transition to advanced level by exposing them to language in more natural contexts. Course materials include newspaper articles and reviews, essays, extracts from novels and film scripts, listening extracts from radio and television (reviews, interviews, awards presentations), and short film sequences. Listening and speaking improve as they hear and practice how English is pronounced at normal speed. Analyzing dialogue makes their conversation more effective. Course materials encourage them to examine cultural aspects of language use in real life. In-class activities give them opportunities to discover and discuss classmates' experiences and opinions, and to compare their own culture and ways of communicating with those represented in film extracts. Writing assignments range from formal letters and essays to mini-scripts. Online grammar and in-class vocabulary work are reinforced through listening, speaking, and writing.

Academic Speaking

Advanced course for non-native speakers of English at the University of Pennsylvania. The course develops students’ ability to communicate effectively in the following academic situations: a) getting information on campus, b) controlling a conversation in the classroom, c)making appointments with teachers and effectively using the time spent in the teacher’s office, d) participating in and leading group discussions, e) giving and receiving advice, f) giving presentations, and g) completing a group project. Common strategies and linguistically and socially appropriate language were taught.

Academic Vocabulary

This course is designed to help high intermediate and advanced students master the words on the Academic Word List (AWL) through reading in context, using corpus tools to analyze word meaning and usage, class games and discussion, and directed individual study.


Evening Courses: Grammar Practice, Oral Presentations, and Academic Spelling Power

Fulbright Pre-Academic Program

This 3-week intensive course prepared Fulbright Scholars from around the world for graduate study in the US. Using Kuhn’s Theory of Scientific Revolutions as content, this course covered academic reading, academic writing conventions, and academic speaking and listening skills.

ISAYA Discovery Course

This is a content-based course with twice-weekly lectures by professors in the Jackson School of International Studies. As the English support instructor, I prepared and delivered an academic skills curriculum to study abroad students from Korea and Japan.

Reading Academic Texts

Advanced course for non-native speakers of English at the University of Pennsylvania. This course helps students improve reading comprehension, study skills (particularly note-taking), critical thinking, library search skills, and their ability to use reading assignments to complete typical university-level assignments. Students read selections from textbooks and journal articles in various academic disciplines and discuss the organization, content, vocabulary and difficult grammatical forms characteristic of academic writing. The course exposes them to academic writing characteristic of the different academic areas.
